# Demo Assets — Public Domain Artworks

> **Purpose**: 3 public domain traditional artworks for VULCA Compare mode demo recordings
> **Usage**: Cultural comparison demonstrations showing how the same evaluation framework yields different scores across cultural traditions

---

## 1. Dunhuang Flying Apsaras (敦煌飞天壁画)

- **Title**: Flying Apsaras, Mogao Cave 320
- **Period**: Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE)
- **Source**: Wikimedia Commons
- **URL**: `https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/Dunhuang_Flying_Apsaras.jpg`
- **License**: Public Domain (artwork created before 1300 CE)
- **Resolution**: ~1600×1200
- **Recommended Crop**: 16:9 landscape, center on the two flying figures
- **Demo Purpose**: Represents Chinese Buddhist art tradition. In Compare mode, should score high on L3 (Cultural Context) through Chinese Xieyi lens, moderate through Western-Classical lens, demonstrating cultural perspective differences.
- **Cultural Significance**: Flying apsaras (飞天) are celestial beings in Buddhist art. Dunhuang murals represent the pinnacle of Silk Road cultural fusion — Chinese brushwork meets Central Asian and Indian iconography.

---

## 2. The Great Wave off Kanagawa (神奈川冲浪里)

- **Title**: Under the Wave off Kanagawa (神奈川沖浪裏)
- **Artist**: Katsushika Hokusai (葛飾北斎)
- **Period**: c. 1831, Edo Period
- **Source**: Wikimedia Commons / Metropolitan Museum of Art (Open Access)
- **URL**: `https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Tsunami_by_hokusai_19th_century.jpg`
- **License**: Public Domain (created before 1900)
- **Resolution**: ~2000×1400
- **Recommended Crop**: Original aspect ratio (roughly 3:2), no crop needed
- **Demo Purpose**: Represents Japanese Ukiyo-e tradition. Strong L2 (Technical Execution) and L4 (Innovation) scores. Comparison with Dunhuang piece shows how technique evaluation criteria differ across Chinese and Japanese traditions.
- **Cultural Significance**: Most iconic work of Japanese woodblock printing. Demonstrates the Ukiyo-e aesthetic of capturing fleeting moments — contrasting with Chinese landscape painting's emphasis on spiritual harmony.

---

## 3. Persian/Islamic Geometric Art

- **Title**: Mihrab (Prayer Niche), 1354-55
- **Source**: The Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access
- **URL**: `https://images.metmuseum.org/CRDImages/is/original/DP235183.jpg`
- **Alt URL**: `https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/Mihrab_%28Prayer_Niche%29%2C_A.H._755-_A.D._1354%E2%80%9355_%28Met%2C_39.20%29.jpg`
- **License**: Public Domain / CC0 (Met Open Access Policy)
- **Resolution**: ~2000×3000
- **Recommended Crop**: 1:1 square focusing on the geometric pattern upper section
- **Demo Purpose**: Represents Islamic art tradition. Should score very high on L2 (Technical Execution) for geometric precision, but evaluation through different cultural lenses reveals contrasting interpretations of abstraction vs. figuration. Key demo point: aniconism principle — no human/animal figures.
- **Cultural Significance**: The mihrab demonstrates Islamic geometric art at its finest — mathematical precision expressing infinite divine order. The absence of figural representation is not a limitation but a theological choice, illustrating why cultural context matters in evaluation.

---

## Usage in Compare Mode Demo

1. Upload all 3 images simultaneously to Compare mode
2. Initial evaluation shows baseline L1-L5 scores for each
3. Switch tradition selector → scores shift dramatically
4. Key insight to demonstrate:
   - Dunhuang piece: High context in Chinese lens, lower in Western
   - Great Wave: High technique universally, but innovation scores vary
   - Mihrab: Technical mastery recognized across cultures, but "creativity" interpretation diverges
5. Radar chart overlay shows three completely different polygons
6. Max Gap column in difference table highlights where cultural disagreement is strongest
